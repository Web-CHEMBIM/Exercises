--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1.1  SPEED AND AVERAGE SPEED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

INTRODUCTION
---------------
Think about the last time you traveled by car, bus, or train. How would you
describe how fast you were moving? In physics, we use the term "speed" to
describe this quantitatively.

> DEFINITION
> Speed is the distance travelled per unit time.

Speed tells us how quickly an object covers a distance. The faster an object
moves, the greater its speed.

FORMULA:

                          distance
    speed = ─────────────────────────────
                            time

Using symbols:

                              s
                    v = ─────────
                              t

where:
    v = speed (in m/s or km/h)
    s = distance (in m or km)
    t = time (in s or h)

SI UNITS:
┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
│ Quantity        │ SI Unit         │ Symbol          │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│ Speed           │ metres per      │ m/s             │
│                 │ second          │                 │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│ Distance        │ metres          │ m               │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│ Time            │ seconds         │ s               │
└─────────────────┴─────────────────┴─────────────────┘

NOTE: Speed is a SCALAR quantity - it has magnitude (size) only, with no
direction.

AVERAGE SPEED
---------------

In real life, objects rarely travel at a constant speed. To describe the
overall motion, we use average speed.

> DEFINITION
> Average speed is the total distance travelled divided by the total time taken.

FORMULA:
                            total distance
    average speed = ─────────────────────────────
                              total time


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WORKED EXAMPLE 1: Calculating Speed
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
A cyclist travels 450 m in 30 s. Calculate the cyclist's speed.

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Identify given values and required quantity
    Given:   s = 450 m
             t = 30 s
    Required: v = ?

Step 2: Select the appropriate equation
             s
    v = ─────────
             t

Step 3: Substitute the values
             450 m
    v = ─────────────
             30 s

Step 4: Calculate
    v = 15 m/s

Step 5: State the answer with correct unit
    The cyclist's speed is 15 m/s.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WORKED EXAMPLE 2: Calculating Distance
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
A train travels at an average speed of 80 km/h for 2.5 hours.
Calculate the total distance traveled.

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Identify given values and required quantity
    Given:   v = 80 km/h
             t = 2.5 h
    Required: s = ?

Step 2: Rearrange the equation to find distance
    From v = s/t, we get:
    s = v × t

Step 3: Substitute the values
    s = 80 km/h × 2.5 h

Step 4: Calculate
    s = 200 km

Step 5: State the answer
    The train travels 200 km.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1.2  VELOCITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

INTRODUCTION
---------------
Imagine two cars traveling at 60 km/h. One is heading north, the other south.
They have the same speed, but they are not moving in the same way! This is
where we need a quantity that includes both speed AND direction.

> DEFINITION
> Velocity is speed in a given direction.

Velocity tells us not just how fast an object is moving, but also the direction
of its motion.

COMPARING SPEED AND VELOCITY:
┌─────────────────┬─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ SPEED           │ VELOCITY                                                    │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Scalar quantity │ Vector quantity                                             │
│ Has magnitude   │ Has magnitude AND direction                                 │
│ only            │                                                             │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ "The car is     │ "The car is traveling at 20 m/s heading north"              │
│ traveling at    │                                                             │
│ 20 m/s"         │                                                             │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Always positive │ Can be positive or negative (depending on direction)        │
└─────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

IMPORTANT:
* A change in speed means velocity changes.
* A change in direction (even at constant speed) also means velocity changes.

DISPLACEMENT VS DISTANCE
--------------------------

When dealing with velocity, we use DISPLACEMENT instead of distance.

> DEFINITION
> Displacement is the distance traveled in a particular direction
> (the straight-line distance from start to finish).

COMPARING DISTANCE AND DISPLACEMENT:
┌─────────────────┬─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ DISTANCE        │ DISPLACEMENT                                                │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Total path      │ Shortest path from start to end point                       │
│ length traveled │ (straight line)                                             │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Scalar          │ Vector                                                      │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Always positive │ Can be positive, negative, or zero                          │
└─────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

2.1.3  ACCELERATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

INTRODUCTION
---------------
When you press the accelerator pedal in a car, the car speeds up. When you
apply the brakes, it slows down. Both are examples of a change in velocity.

> DEFINITION
> Acceleration is the change in velocity per unit time.
> (Or: Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity.)

FORMULA:

                        change in velocity
    acceleration = ────────────────────────────
                           time taken

Using symbols:

                         Δv        v - u
                a = ─────────  = ─────────
                         Δt           t

where:
    a  = acceleration (in m/s²)
    Δv = change in velocity (in m/s)
    v  = final velocity (in m/s)
    u  = initial velocity (in m/s)
    t  = time taken (in s)

SI UNITS:
    Acceleration is measured in metres per second squared (m/s²).

NOTE: Acceleration is a VECTOR quantity - it has both magnitude and direction.


UNDERSTANDING ACCELERATION VALUES
-----------------------------------

POSITIVE ACCELERATION:
    * Velocity is increasing
    * Object is speeding up
    * Example: a = +2 m/s² means velocity increases by 2 m/s every second

NEGATIVE ACCELERATION (DECELERATION):
    * Velocity is decreasing
    * Object is slowing down
    * Example: a = -3 m/s² means velocity decreases by 3 m/s every second

> DEFINITION
> Deceleration is a negative acceleration.
> It means the object is slowing down.

CAUTION:
Negative acceleration does NOT always mean slowing down!
If an object moves in the negative direction and has negative acceleration,
it is actually speeding up! The key is whether acceleration and velocity
point in the same direction.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
A car accelerates from rest to 24 m/s in 8.0 s. Calculate the acceleration.

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Identify given values and required quantity
    Given:   u = 0 m/s (rest)
             v = 24 m/s
             t = 8.0 s
    Required: a = ?

Step 2: Select the appropriate equation
                 v - u
    a = ─────────────────
                    t

Step 3: Substitute the values
                 24 - 0
    a = ─────────────────
                  8.0

Step 4: Calculate
    a = 3.0 m/s²

Step 5: State the answer
    The acceleration of the car is 3.0 m/s².

    Given:   u = 15 m/s

                 v - u
    a = ─────────────────

2.2.1  DISTANCE-TIME GRAPHS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A distance-time graph is used to describe the motion of an object and calculate its speed.
  - The x-axis represents TIME.
  - The y-axis represents DISTANCE.

INTERPRETING THE SHAPE:

Shape                     | Meaning
--------------------------|--------------------------------------------
Horizontal line           | Object is AT REST (stationary). Distance does not change.
Straight diagonal line    | Object is moving at CONSTANT SPEED.
Curved line (concave up)  | Object is ACCELERATING (speeding up). Gradient increases.
Curved line (concave down)| Object is DECELERATING (slowing down). Gradient decreases.

- **Steeper slope** = Faster speed.
- **Shallower slope** = Slower speed.

CALCULATING SPEED FROM A DISTANCE-TIME GRAPH:

The GRADIENT (slope) of a distance-time graph equals the SPEED.

                        change in y      distance
    Speed = Gradient = ──────────────────────  = ───────────────
                        change in x         time

    v = Δy / Δx

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WORKED EXAMPLE 5: Speed from a Distance-Time Graph
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
A distance-time graph shows a train journey. The train travels at a constant speed, covering 8000 m in 360 s.
Calculate the speed of the train.

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Identify that speed is the gradient of the distance-time graph.
    Speed = Gradient = Δy / Δx

Step 2: Substitute the values.
    Δy (distance) = 8000 m
    Δx (time) = 360 s

    v = 8000 / 360

Step 3: Calculate.
    v = 22.2 m/s

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WORKED EXAMPLE 6: Interpreting a Distance-Time Graph
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
A student walks to a park, sits on a bench to read, and then runs home.
- Section A: Straight line with moderate slope.
- Section B: Horizontal line.
- Section C: Straight line with steep slope.

a) What does Section B represent?
b) Which section represents the fastest speed?

SOLUTION:

a) Section B is a horizontal line. This means the distance is not changing.
   The student is AT REST (sitting on the bench).

b) Section C has the steepest slope.
   A steeper slope means a higher gradient, which means a higher speed.
   Therefore, Section C represents the fastest speed (running home).

2.2.2  SPEED-TIME GRAPHS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A speed-time graph is used to describe the speed of an object and calculate its acceleration and distance travelled.
  - The x-axis represents TIME.
  - The y-axis represents SPEED.

INTERPRETING THE SHAPE:

Shape                     | Meaning
--------------------------|--------------------------------------------
Horizontal line (y > 0)   | Moving at CONSTANT SPEED (Acceleration = 0).
Horizontal line (y = 0)   | Object is AT REST (Stationary).
Straight line going UP    | CONSTANT ACCELERATION (Speeding up).
Straight line going DOWN  | CONSTANT DECELERATION (Slowing down).
Curved line               | CHANGING ACCELERATION.

- **Positive Gradient** = Acceleration.
- **Negative Gradient** = Deceleration.

KEY CALCULATIONS:

1.  CALCULATING ACCELERATION:
    The GRADIENT of a speed-time graph equals the ACCELERATION.

                            change in speed      Δv
    Acceleration = Gradient = ─────────────────────────────────  = ────
                                time taken         Δt

2.  CALCULATING DISTANCE:
    The AREA UNDER a speed-time graph equals the DISTANCE TRAVELLED.

    - For a rectangle: Area = base × height
    - For a triangle:  Area = ½ × base × height
    - Total Distance = Sum of all areas.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WORKED EXAMPLE 7: Distance from a Speed-Time Graph
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
A car travels at 10 m/s for 5 s, then accelerates to 20 m/s in 5 s.
Calculate the total distance travelled.

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Split the area under the graph into shapes.
    - Area A (Rectangle): 0 to 5 s. Speed = 10 m/s.
    - Area B (Trapezium or Rectangle + Triangle): 5 to 10 s. Speed 10 to 20 m/s.

Step 2: Calculate Area A.
    Area A = base × height = 5 s × 10 m/s = 50 m.

Step 3: Calculate Area B.
    Split into a rectangle (5s × 10 m/s) and a triangle (5s × 10 m/s).
    Rectangle Area = 5 × 10 = 50 m.
    Triangle Area = ½ × 5 × (20 - 10) = ½ × 5 × 10 = 25 m.
    Total Area B = 50 + 25 = 75 m.

Step 4: Total Distance.
    Distance = Area A + Area B = 50 m + 75 m = 125 m.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.2.3  NON-UNIFORM ACCELERATION (EXTENDED)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

When the speed-time graph is a CURVE, the acceleration is changing.
- **Increasing Gradient** (curve getting steeper) = Increasing Acceleration.
- **Decreasing Gradient** (curve flattening) = Decreasing Acceleration.

CALCULATING INSTANTANEOUS ACCELERATION:
To find the acceleration at a specific time on a curve:
1.  Draw a **TANGENT** to the curve at that specific time point.
    (A tangent is a straight line that touches the curve at only one point and matches its slope).
2.  Calculate the **GRADIENT** of the tangent.

    Acceleration = Gradient of Tangent = Δv / Δt

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WORKED EXAMPLE 8: Acceleration from a Tangent
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
A skydiver falls with changing acceleration. A speed-time graph is plotted.
Calculate the acceleration at t = 5 s.

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Draw a tangent.
    Draw a straight line touching the curve at t = 5 s.

Step 2: Calculate the gradient of the tangent.
    Pick two points on the tangent line (not necessarily on the curve).
    Point 1: (0.75 s, 20 m/s)
    Point 2: (9.75 s, 58 m/s)

    Gradient = (58 - 20) / (9.75 - 0.75)
             = 38 / 9.0
             = 4.2

Step 3: State the answer.
    Acceleration = 4.2 m/s²

2.3.1  ACCELERATION DUE TO GRAVITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

> KEY STATEMENT
> The acceleration of free fall, g, for an object near to the surface of the
> Earth is approximately constant and is approximately 9.8 m/s².

This means:
  - All objects, regardless of their mass, fall with the same acceleration
    (when air resistance is negligible).
  - Every second, the velocity of a falling object increases by about 9.8 m/s.

    g ≈ 9.8 m/s²

EXAMPLE: Falling object
If you drop a ball from rest:
  - After 1 second: speed = 9.8 m/s
  - After 2 seconds: speed = 19.6 m/s
  - After 3 seconds: speed = 29.4 m/s

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.3.2  MOTION WITH AIR RESISTANCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

In the real world, air resistance (drag) opposes the motion of falling objects.

MOTION OF A FALLING OBJECT IN AIR:

1.  START: Object falls, speed is low, air resistance is small.
    Net force is large → acceleration is close to g (9.8 m/s²).

2.  MIDDLE: As speed increases, air resistance increases.
    Net force decreases → acceleration decreases.

3.  END (TERMINAL VELOCITY): Air resistance equals weight.
    Net force is zero → acceleration is zero.
    The object now falls at a constant maximum speed.

> DEFINITION
> Terminal velocity is the maximum constant velocity reached by a falling
> object when the air resistance (drag) equals the object's weight.
