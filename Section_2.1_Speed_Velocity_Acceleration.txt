================================================================================
2.1.1  SPEED AND AVERAGE SPEED
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
INTRODUCTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Think about the last time you traveled by car, bus, or train. How would you 
describe how fast you were moving? When we say a car is traveling at 60 km/h, 
what does that actually mean?

In everyday life, we use the word "speed" to describe how fast something is 
moving. In physics, speed has a precise definition that helps us calculate and 
compare the motion of different objects.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS SPEED?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

> DEFINITION
> Speed is the distance travelled per unit time.

Speed tells us how quickly an object covers a distance. The faster an object 
moves, the greater its speed.

FORMULA:
                         distance
    speed = ─────────────────────────────
                           time

Using symbols:

                              s
                    v = ─────────
                              t

where:
    v = speed (in m/s or km/h)
    s = distance (in m or km)
    t = time (in s or h)


SI UNITS:
┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
│ Quantity        │ SI Unit         │ Symbol          │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│ Speed           │ metres per      │ m/s             │
│                 │ second          │                 │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│ Distance        │ metres          │ m               │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│ Time            │ seconds         │ s               │
└─────────────────┴─────────────────┴─────────────────┘

NOTE: Speed is a SCALAR quantity - it has magnitude (size) only, with no 
direction. When we say a car is traveling at 20 m/s, we are describing its 
speed.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
AVERAGE SPEED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

In real life, objects rarely travel at a constant speed. A car speeds up, slows 
down, and sometimes stops. To describe the overall motion, we use average speed.

> DEFINITION
> Average speed is the total distance travelled divided by the total time taken.

FORMULA:
                            total distance
    average speed = ─────────────────────────────
                              total time

Using symbols:

                             s_total
                    v̄ = ─────────────
                             t_total


EXAMPLE: A journey where speed changes

A car travels 100 km in the first hour and 80 km in the next hour.

Total distance = 100 + 80 = 180 km
Total time = 1 + 1 = 2 hours

Average speed = 180 km / 2 h = 90 km/h

Even though the car traveled at different speeds during the journey, its 
average speed was 90 km/h.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WORKED EXAMPLE 1: Calculating Speed
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
A cyclist travels 450 m in 30 s. Calculate the cyclist's speed.

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Identify given values and required quantity
    Given:   s = 450 m
             t = 30 s
    Required: v = ?

Step 2: Select the appropriate equation
             s
    v = ─────────
             t

Step 3: Substitute the values
             450 m
    v = ─────────────
             30 s

Step 4: Calculate
    v = 15 m/s

Step 5: State the answer with correct unit
    The cyclist's speed is 15 m/s.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WORKED EXAMPLE 2: Calculating Distance
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
A train travels at an average speed of 80 km/h for 2.5 hours. 
Calculate the total distance traveled.

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Identify given values and required quantity
    Given:   v = 80 km/h
             t = 2.5 h
    Required: s = ?

Step 2: Rearrange the equation to find distance
    From v = s/t, we get:
    s = v × t

Step 3: Substitute the values
    s = 80 km/h × 2.5 h

Step 4: Calculate
    s = 200 km

Step 5: State the answer
    The train travels 200 km.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WORKED EXAMPLE 3: Calculating Time
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
An athlete runs at an average speed of 8.0 m/s. How long does it take the 
athlete to run 400 m?

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Identify given values and required quantity
    Given:   v = 8.0 m/s
             s = 400 m
    Required: t = ?

Step 2: Rearrange the equation to find time
    From v = s/t, we get:
             s
    t = ─────────
             v

Step 3: Substitute the values
             400 m
    t = ─────────────
            8.0 m/s

Step 4: Calculate
    t = 50 s

Step 5: State the answer
    The athlete takes 50 s to run 400 m.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
UNIT CONVERSION REMINDER
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

When calculating, always check that your units are consistent!

COMMON CONVERSIONS:
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ km/h to m/s:    divide by 3.6      │  Example: 72 km/h ÷ 3.6 = 20 m/s    │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ m/s to km/h:    multiply by 3.6    │  Example: 15 m/s × 3.6 = 54 km/h    │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ km to m:        multiply by 1000   │  Example: 2.5 km × 1000 = 2500 m    │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ min to s:       multiply by 60     │  Example: 5 min × 60 = 300 s        │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ h to s:         multiply by 3600   │  Example: 2 h × 3600 = 7200 s       │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

2.1.2  VELOCITY
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
INTRODUCTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Imagine two cars traveling at 60 km/h. One is heading north, and the other is 
heading south. Do they have the same speed? Yes - both travel 60 km every hour.

But are they moving in the same way? No - they are traveling in opposite 
directions! This is where we need a quantity that includes both speed AND 
direction.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS VELOCITY?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

> DEFINITION
> Velocity is speed in a given direction.

Velocity tells us not just how fast an object is moving, but also the direction 
of its motion.

COMPARING SPEED AND VELOCITY:
┌─────────────────┬─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ SPEED           │ VELOCITY                                                    │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Scalar quantity │ Vector quantity                                             │
│ Has magnitude   │ Has magnitude AND direction                                 │
│ only            │                                                             │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ "The car is     │ "The car is traveling at 20 m/s heading north"              │
│ traveling at    │                                                             │
│ 20 m/s"         │                                                             │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Always positive │ Can be positive or negative (depending on direction)        │
└─────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

IMPORTANT: 
• A change in speed means velocity changes
• A change in direction (even at constant speed) also means velocity changes

EXAMPLES:
1. A car going around a roundabout at constant speed of 30 km/h:
   - Speed: constant (30 km/h)
   - Velocity: changing (direction keeps changing)

2. A ball thrown vertically upward:
   - Speed: changes (slows down going up, speeds up coming down)
   - Velocity: changes (direction reverses at the top)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DISPLACEMENT VS DISTANCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

When dealing with velocity, we use DISPLACEMENT instead of distance.

> DEFINITION
> Displacement is the distance traveled in a particular direction 
> (the straight-line distance from start to finish).

COMPARING DISTANCE AND DISPLACEMENT:
┌─────────────────┬─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ DISTANCE        │ DISPLACEMENT                                                │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Total path      │ Shortest path from start to end point                       │
│ length traveled │ (straight line)                                             │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Scalar          │ Vector                                                      │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Always positive │ Can be positive, negative, or zero                          │
└─────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

EXAMPLE:
A person walks 4 m east, then 3 m north.

    Distance traveled = 4 + 3 = 7 m
    Displacement = 5 m (at an angle) [by Pythagoras: √(4² + 3²) = 5]

If a person walks in a complete circle back to the starting point:
    Distance = circumference of circle
    Displacement = 0 (no net change in position)

2.1.3  ACCELERATION
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
INTRODUCTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

When you press the accelerator pedal in a car, the car speeds up. When you 
apply the brakes, it slows down. Both of these are examples of acceleration - 
but what exactly does this mean in physics?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS ACCELERATION?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

> DEFINITION
> Acceleration is the change in velocity per unit time.
> (Or: Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity.)

Acceleration tells us how quickly velocity is changing.

FORMULA:
                        change in velocity
    acceleration = ────────────────────────────
                           time taken

Using symbols:

                         Δv        v - u
                a = ─────────  = ─────────
                         Δt           t

where:
    a = acceleration (in m/s²)
    Δv = change in velocity (in m/s)
    v = final velocity (in m/s)
    u = initial velocity (in m/s)
    t = time taken (in s)

SI UNITS:
    Acceleration is measured in metres per second squared (m/s²)
    
    This unit makes sense: if velocity changes by 1 m/s every second,
    the acceleration is 1 m/s².

NOTE: Acceleration is a VECTOR quantity - it has both magnitude and direction.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
UNDERSTANDING ACCELERATION VALUES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

POSITIVE ACCELERATION:
    • Velocity is increasing
    • Object is speeding up (in the positive direction)
    • Example: a = +2 m/s² means velocity increases by 2 m/s every second

NEGATIVE ACCELERATION (DECELERATION):
    • Velocity is decreasing
    • Object is slowing down (in the positive direction)
    • Example: a = -3 m/s² means velocity decreases by 3 m/s every second

> DEFINITION
> Deceleration is a negative acceleration.
> It means the object is slowing down.

CAUTION: 
Negative acceleration does NOT always mean slowing down!
• If an object moves in the negative direction and has negative acceleration,
  it is actually speeding up!
• The key is: acceleration in the same direction as velocity = speeding up
                acceleration opposite to velocity = slowing down

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WORKED EXAMPLE 4: Calculating Acceleration
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
A car accelerates from rest to 24 m/s in 8.0 s. Calculate the acceleration.

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Identify given values and required quantity
    Given:   u = 0 m/s (rest)
             v = 24 m/s
             t = 8.0 s
    Required: a = ?

Step 2: Select the appropriate equation
                 v - u
    a = ─────────────────
                    t

Step 3: Substitute the values
                 24 - 0
    a = ─────────────────
                  8.0

Step 4: Calculate
    a = 3.0 m/s²

Step 5: State the answer
    The acceleration of the car is 3.0 m/s².

    (This means the car's velocity increases by 3.0 m/s every second)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WORKED EXAMPLE 5: Calculating Deceleration
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
A cyclist traveling at 15 m/s applies the brakes and comes to rest in 6.0 s.
Calculate the deceleration.

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Identify given values and required quantity
    Given:   u = 15 m/s
             v = 0 m/s (comes to rest)
             t = 6.0 s
    Required: a = ?

Step 2: Select the appropriate equation
                 v - u
    a = ─────────────────
                    t

Step 3: Substitute the values
                 0 - 15
    a = ─────────────────
                  6.0

Step 4: Calculate
    a = -2.5 m/s²

Step 5: State the answer
    The acceleration is -2.5 m/s² (or deceleration of 2.5 m/s²).

    (The negative sign indicates the cyclist is slowing down)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WORKED EXAMPLE 6: Finding Final Velocity
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM:
A train accelerates at 0.80 m/s² for 25 s. If the initial velocity is 5.0 m/s,
calculate the final velocity.

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Identify given values and required quantity
    Given:   u = 5.0 m/s
             a = 0.80 m/s²
             t = 25 s
    Required: v = ?

Step 2: Rearrange the equation to find final velocity
    From a = (v - u)/t, we get:
    v = u + at

Step 3: Substitute the values
    v = 5.0 + (0.80 × 25)

Step 4: Calculate
    v = 5.0 + 20
    v = 25 m/s

Step 5: State the answer
    The final velocity of the train is 25 m/s.
